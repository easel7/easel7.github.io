                        1.Introduction to axion

                        In the early 1970s, physics had a symmetry problem. According to the Standard Model (SM), CP-violation should feed into the strong interaction as the weak interaction does. But experiments show that, unlike the weak interaction, the strong interaction obeys the CP symmetry perfectly. In 1977, physicists Roberto Peccei and Helen Quinn [@peccei1977cp;@peccei1977constraints] found a solution: a a mechanism that, if it existed, would cause the strong force to obey this symmetry and refine SM. Shortly after, Frank Wilczek and Steven Weinberg [@weinberg1978new;@wilczek1978problem] realized that this mechanism creates an entirely new particle, Axion. Nowadays, Axions and axion-like particles (ALPs) are a compelling class of theoretical pseudo-scalar particles to serve as solutions to lingering problem in particle physics as well as the dark matter candidate.

                        1.2 Axion as a non-WIMP dark matter candidate

                        In the early universe, cold axion populations may arise from vacuum realignment [@preskill1983cosmology;@abbott1983cosmological;@dine1983not] in non-thermal mechanisms. Which mechanisms contribute depends on whether the breaks before or after inflation. Although the axions have tiny mass, they are likely to produced in prodigious numbers and behave as a cold matter and never achieve thermal equilibrium with the rest of the universe, which could provide the required dark matter energy density.

                        According to Eq.(4), the interactions between axion and ordinary particles, like axion-photon coupling strength , are inversely proportional to , and they are highly suppressed if is sufficiently large. In other words, axions are rather "invisible", for instance, the only significant long-range interactions are gravity. Hence, axions have the competence of gather the ordinary matter to contribute the large-scale structure in the universe.

                        These two features I mentioned above make the axion suitable for explaining the nature of dark matter. Around the time the axion was proposed, physicists were developing a theory called Supersymmetry, which called for a partner for every known particle. The newly proposed dark matter candidate called Weakly Interacting Massive Particles (WIMPs) which fit beautifully with the theory of SuperSymmetry (SUSY) [@jungman1996supersymmetric], making physicists all but certain they’d both be discovered. But hard evidence of both WIMPS and SUSY has yet to appear leading severe constraints on the WIMP parameter space. Differed from the WIMP, axion may be just a first representative of a new dark matter family of so called Weakly Interacting Sub-eV Particles (WISPs).

                        1.3 axion and axion-like particle (ALP)

                        1.3.2 Constraints on axion and ALP by experiments

                        Several ultrasensitive experiments based on axion/AyP-photon mixing are conducted to capture the axion and ALPs, but still remain in restricting the parameter space of mass and coupling strength [@graham2015experimental;@irastorza2018new]. In this section, we will briefly review some experiments in accordance with the experiment situations.

                        1.3.2.1 Terrestrial Experiments
                        • ADMX and ADMX-HF experiments - Cavity microwave experiment

                        In 1983, Pierre Sikivie [@abbott1983cosmological] proposed a method which utilize the axion-photon conversion in the presence of electromagnetic field. Axion Dark Matter eXperiment (ADMX) and ADMX-HF (High Frequency) were conducted to search the axion dark matter constituting the Milky Way halo. ADMX and ADMX-HF belong to the cavity microwave experiment which is designed to convert axions into a monochromatic microwave signals in a high-Q microwave cavity under a strong magnetic field.

                        Fig2. Schematic of the microwave cavity search for dark matter axions. Axions resonantly convert to a quasi-monochromatic microwave signal in a high-Q cavity in a strong magnetic field; the signal is extracted from the cavity by an antenna, amplified, mixed down to the audio range, and the power spectrum calculated by a FFT. Possible fine structure on top of the thermalized axion spectrum would reveal important information about the formation of our galaxy. [@graham2015experimental]

                        The microwave cavity experiment is making excellent progress in the search for dark matter axions in the range and may be plausibly extended up to .

                        1.4 The cosmic opacity problem

                        According to the current understanding, the flux of very-high-energy (VHE) gamma rays from distant sources is attenuated in an energy-dependent way by the interaction with background photons in the Universe [@fazio1970predicted]. Specifically, the dominant process for the absorption of a photon of energy is pair-production . Actually, becomes maximal for a background photon energy . In the energy interval between 100 GeV and 1 TeV, the absorption is dominated by the interaction with infrared/optical photons of the metagalactic radiation field (MRF), often called extragalactic background light (EBL) [@nikishov1961absorption;@gould1967opacity;@fazio1970predicted], while the interaction with the CMB becomes dominant at .

                        Imaging Atmospheric Cherenkov Telescopes (IACTs) are providing us with an impressive amount of information about the Universe in the energy interval from to . Observations carried out by these IACTs concern gamma-ray sources over an extremely wide interval of distances, ranging from the parsec scale for Galactic objects up to the Gigaparsec scale for the fartest detected blazar 3C279. The detection result of blazar 3C279 by MAGIC has confirmed previous indications by H.E.S.S. that the universe is more transparent to very-high-energy gamma rays than currently thought [@albert2008very;@roncadelli2009new]. And unexpected gamma-ray detection of the PKS 1222+216 by MAGIC [magic2012results] can be explained consistently with the simultaneous results by Fermi/LAT – within a standard blazar model by adding the new assumption that inside the source photons can oscillate into ALPs [@tavecchio2012evidence].

                        Moreover, the unphysical spectral features in absorbed spectra (pair production) for an ensemble of blazars showed up precisely at the energies for which the correction for the absorption on EBL became crucial [@horns2012indications;@rubtsov2014breaks]. This is not the only case: the anomalous transparency of the universe for high-energy gamma rays arouse discussing, and Korochkin [@korochkin2019search] disfavored the absence of anomalous distance-dependent spectral features with the modest statistical significance of according the subsample from IACTs and Fermi-LAT. In another words, the gamma ray spectra behavior from blazars are not perfectly consistent with the EBL/CMB absorption-only model. This discrepancy has been tentatively interpreted as a manifestation of axion-like particles [@de2007evidence;@kartavtsev2017extragalactic] which is reducing gamma-ray opacity with photon-ALP oscillations.

                        As I have mentioned above, the possible way to resolve this cosmic opacity problem is the theoretical-predicted particle, axion and ALP. According to Section 1.3.1, ALPs can couple with photons in the presence of the magnetic field and go through the opaque medium. If ALP-photon conversion exists, high energy gamma rays may convert into ALPs in the extragalactic source magnetic field domain, then propagate through the opaque interstellar medium, and convert back to high energy photons in the magnetic field of the Milky Way. Indeed, ALPs substantially enhance the photon survival probability of a beam emitted by a remote astrophysical source through the mechanism of photon-ALP oscillations in certain circumstances. Fortunately, gamma rays emitted from remote astrophysical sources is rather sensitive to the EBL/CMB absorption.
